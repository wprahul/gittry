Social Security benefits are payments made to qualified retirees and disabled people, and to their spouses, children, and survivors. The official name for Social Security is “the Old-Age, Survivors, and Disability Insurance (OASDI) Program in the United States.” It refers to a comprehensive federal benefits program designed to provide partial replacement income for older workers and their spouse, those whose spouse or qualifying ex-spouse have died, and the disabled. In some specific circumstances, it also supports children and beneficiaries. The history of Social Security benefits could be traced back to 1935, when President Franklin Roosevelt signed the original Social Security Act into law. All of these benefits are determined by a specific set of criteria issued by the Social Security Administration (SSA). Social Security benefits work with the payroll taxes. The Internal Revenue Service collects tax deposits and formally entrusts them to the Social Security Trust Fund, which is made up of the Old-Age and Survivors Insurance Trust Fund and the Disability Insurance Trust Fund. In other words, Social Security benefits are funded by the taxes. Individuals qualify for Social Security old age (or retirement) benefits by paying into the program during their working years. For example, full insurance is based on accumulating 40 credits from covered wages, and a worker can earn up to four credits a year. Starting in 2020, one credit is awarded for every $1,410 in earnings. Besides, this amount is adjusted annually to keep up with inflation, which means one credit might be worth more in the future years during an inflation.

In other words, Social Security benefits are funded by the taxes. Individuals qualify for Social Security old age (or retirement) benefits by paying into the program during their working years.
